THE SCIENCE OF SLEEP

IScientists have long recognized that sleep is one of the most essential human needs. Yet in today's fast-paced world, millions of people — particularly teenagers — suffer from serious sleep deprivation1. Research shows that lack of rest affects concentration, emotional stability, and physical health. Despite growing concern about this issue, the number of people sleeping fewer than seven hours a night continues to rise.

IIStudies have identified several important benefits of quality sleep. First, sleep allows the brain to organize and store cognitive2 information learned throughout the day. In addition, the body uses this time to repair cells and strengthen the immune3 system. However, experts point out that modern lifestyles frequently disrupt these natural processes, preventing many people from gaining their full benefits.

IIIAmong the biggest challenges to healthy sleep is the use of digital screens. Devices such as phones and computers emit4 a type of light that keeps the brain alert5 long after bedtime. Dr. Maya Cohen, a sleep researcher at the University of Haifa, argues that the considerable delay between screen exposure and sleep onset6 is largely underestimated by both parents and educators. In schools where phone use was restricted before bedtime, students reported sleeping significantly better.

IVGood sleep also produces important social benefits. Students who sleep well demonstrate greater flexibility in problem-solving and stronger emotional resilience. Some schools in Finland and the United States have responded by shifting their start times to later in the morning. Experts argue that such institutional changes can help young people escape the damaging cycle of sleep deprivation.

VScientists believe that sleep research will continue to expand. Although technology alone cannot completely solve the problem, it can contribute to developing more efficient solutions. If governments, schools, and families work together to establish healthier daily routines, sleep deprivation may gradually become a consequence of the past rather than a challenge of the present.

GLOSSARY

  • 2. cognitive — related to thinking and learning
  • 3. immune — protected against illness
  • 4. emit — to produce or send out
  • 5. alert — awake and paying attention
  • 6. onset — the beginning of something
